From Whiteboards to Integrated Workflows: A Modern Journey in Incident Management

05 May 2025 • 4 min read

In the early days of security operations, incident response revolved around a single room, a whiteboard, and a handful of analysts who could shout updates across a desk. Evidence lived on network shares, timelines were scribbled with dry-erase pens, and coordination was effortless—until teams began working across time zones. Suddenly hand-offs spanned continents, leadership expected real-time updates, and a process designed for a boutique crew strained under global scale. The first attempt to digitise the war-room came in the form of a proprietary incident-response platform intended to replace paper logs. On paper it seemed ideal: workflow templates, evidence linking, and a built-in reporting engine. In practice, three roadblocks emerged. Licensing was sold per user, so only the core responders could log in; everyone else waited for status emails. Integration with the security information and event management (SIEM) platform and threat-intelligence feeds was either clumsy or nonexistent, forcing analysts to copy indicators by hand. Worst of all, the interface slowed to a crawl during live incidents, turning a promised “single pane of glass” into a single point of frustration. The tool survived, but only as a repository for post-incident documentation. Frustration with the proprietary solution led the team to an open-source platform. Because it was licence-free, every responder—and every stakeholder—could collaborate in the same space. The platform shipped with connectors that enriched artefacts automatically, so as soon as a hash or domain landed in the case, additional context appeared in seconds. Chat-ops integration meant that the conversation around the incident, complete with time stamps and threaded actions, was preserved as evidence. A cryptographic vault hashed every upload, providing immutability and defensible chain-of-custody. The honeymoon period, however, revealed two familiar truths about open source: bugs arrive unannounced, and expertise can bottleneck around a single engineer. Report generators defaulted to the wrong language, on-premise deployment limited the use of cloud extensions, and when the resident expert took leave, patches waited in the queue. With operational maturity rising, leadership began asking for a platform that could handle incident response, service management, change control, and audit under one umbrella—supported by a vendor that answers the phone at three a.m. The natural next step was an enterprise IT-service-management suite equipped with a major-incident module. Because it shares data with the broader service desk, security incidents automatically inherit asset information, maintenance windows, and ownership details. Robust APIs allow alerts from the SIEM to open tickets instantly, while chat integrations preserve the conversational timeline without manual exports. The pilot environment is live, and the final decision—whether to migrate years of historical cases into the new system or archive them for compliance—will follow a data-retention workshop later this quarter. Not every challenge involves ransomware or zero-days. A recent spate of unexplained peripheral disconnects, traced in part to a new operating-system build, produced a flurry of tickets that competed for the same incident-response capacity as higher-severity events. The episode reminded the team that routine platform issues travel the same pipelines as headline breaches, and that a mature process must scale gracefully in both directions.

lessons learned

  • Plan for growth, not the current headcount.
  • Demand integration, or risk losing time and context.
  • Treat chats as evidence, with export and retention plans.
Incident management is not a destination but an operating rhythm. The evolution from dry-erase scribbles to an open-source workhorse and, soon, to an integrated enterprise suite shows that tools matter, yet adaptability matters more. The wisest advice from the journey is deceptively simple: choose the platform that makes the right action the easiest action, and keep your playbooks portable—you will need them when the next solution comes along.
